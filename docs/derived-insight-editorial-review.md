# Derived Insight Editorial Review

## Purpose

Review the current derived-insight output as an editorial layer rather than treating every strong computation as a public claim.

This review is about:
- public wording
- legibility
- historical honesty
- deciding which internal cross-dataset cousin candidates are worth holding for later release

## Public prompt review

### USA

The first USA pilot is now strong enough to stay public, but needed one clear correction:
- internal family names such as `mobilised settlement` were too academic or too compressed for public prompts

Public prompt rule now:
- family prompts should say what the pattern feels like, not what the internal clustering system calls it

Examples:
- `This era is one of the moments when order is rebuilt under pressure.`
- `This era is one of the moments when fast change starts to fray trust.`

Relationship prompts should also stay plain:
- `tends to rise with`
- `tends to pull away from`
- `often moves before`

That language is not analytically perfect, but it is much more readable in-product.

## Release posture

Keep public now:
- USA pilot prompts
- USA families / relationships / outliers in Insights Lab

Keep internal for now:
- Britain and France public prompts
- public cross-dataset cousins
- public echo-support judgements

## Promotion decision

The current recommendation is:
- keep USA public
- promote the same public insight surface to Britain and France next
- do not jump straight from USA public pilot to public cross-dataset cousins

Reason:
- the product should become cohesive across the three live datasets before it starts making public across-country claims
- this follows the new rollout rule: promote the pilot into the live system before opening a new public scope

## Recommended next release

If one cross-dataset cousin is eventually made public first, it should be:
- `USA 2001-2025` and `France 1981-2025`

Reason:
- the similarity is strong
- the story is easy to grasp
- the public can feel the late-modern rhyme quickly

## Constraint

Do not surface a cross-dataset cousin publicly unless:
- the pairing is statistically strong
- the wording is plain
- the resemblance is historically legible without a paragraph of explanation
