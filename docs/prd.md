# PRD — The History Loom

## 1. Product vision

Create an interactive historical experience that allows people to see history not merely as a sequence of events, but as recurring structures, pressures, releases, values, moods, gains, losses, and human consequences.

The user should come away feeling:
- “I can see history differently now.”
- “These eras are not identical, but they rhyme.”
- “Events make more sense when I can see what built beneath them.”

## 2. Core design principle

The product must balance two truths:

### Truth A — Humans crave pattern
Users are drawn to repeatable chunks, phases, and recurrence.

### Truth B — History is not a clock
The product must avoid naive determinism. It should present cycle lengths and structural patterns as lenses or hypotheses, while also allowing exploration of non-rigid pressure systems.

## 3. Product thesis

History becomes more legible when explored through three linked ideas:
- **Periodicity** — comparable chunks of time under a chosen lens
- **Pressure** — inequality, legitimacy, disruption, cohesion, strain, belief, insecurity, etc.
- **Echo** — structurally similar moments across time

## 4. Audience

### Primary audience
- curious general users
- history enthusiasts
- educators
- writers, researchers, strategists

### Audience expectations
Users will expect:
- visual elegance
- clarity
- meaningful depth
- a sense of discovery
- the ability to compare eras quickly

## 5. Experience goals

The product should enable users to:
1. See repeatable historical structures through fixed time chunks.
2. Understand causal build-up through pressure accumulation and release.
3. Compare different eras using common dimensions.
4. Explore the human condition at multiple scales.
5. See what each era created and what it destroyed.
6. Discover echoes across time based on structure, not just subject.
7. Move from calm overview to rich detail without losing coherence.

## 6. Non-goals

The product is not trying to:
- explain all history with one deterministic cycle
- provide a complete encyclopedic history of everything
- overwhelm users with text and data
- reduce history to wars, rulers, and dates alone

## 7. Core modes

### Mode 1 — Clock
History shown as equal-sized period chunks under a selected lens.

### Mode 2 — Pressure
History shown as rising and falling forces that shape events.

### Mode 3 — Echo
History shown as structurally similar moments linked across eras.

## 8. Multi-scale design

Every selected period should be explorable at four scales:
- personal
- local
- national/state
- global/civilizational

This prevents the experience collapsing into only elite or geopolitical history.

## 9. Signature interactions

### Show me another time this felt the same
Find another era with similar structural conditions.

### What did this era make possible?
Reveal gains, freedoms, inventions, cooperation, and cultural emergence.

### What did this era quietly destroy?
Reveal losses, displacements, rigidities, simplifications, and erosion.

### Pressure cascade
Select a pressure and reveal events and lived consequences shaped by it.

## 10. Suggested lens options
- generational lens (~25–30 years)
- civic/social lens (~75–100 years)
- long secular lens (~150–250 years)
- custom lens

## 11. Initial scope recommendation

This starter pack uses:
- **Britain, 1066 to 2025**
- **12 equal 80-year periods** for the initial seed lens

This is a buildable first lens, not a claim that Britain “really is” an 80-year machine.

## 12. Success criteria

The product succeeds if users can:
- grasp the overview within seconds
- discover at least one meaningful cross-era pattern
- understand at least one event as a pressure outcome
- see both gains and losses in a period
- feel history is more legible, not more flattened

## 13. Product mantra
- Not a timeline. A loom.
- Not just events. Conditions.
- Not just repetition. Pressure and release.
- Not just the past. The shape of human recurrence.
